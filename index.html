Buy Training

Build literacy in isolation from real work

→

Hire Consultants

Redesign workflows in PowerPoint decks

→

Procure Tools

Cycle repeats — dependency compounds

Practitioners learn concepts but not application. They return to their desks and revert to old patterns because the training wasn't anchored to the work they actually do.

Same

The AI capability gap is widening between organizations that built internal fluency early and those still outsourcing the learning curve.

The strategic choice isn't whether to adopt AI. It's whether to own the capability or rent it indefinitely.

A Different Way

Learning in Real Work Context

Fluency is only developed when instruction is delivered in real work context, not in isolation. Learning, workflow change, and tool creation don't happen in sequence. They happen as one activity.

◆ Instruction

AI fundamentals, prompt engineering, agent building

Practitioners don't learn "how to write effective prompts" in the abstract. They learn by prompting AI to draft real deliverables they're actively working on — then refine, iterate, and build agents that codify their best approaches.

◆ Workflow Change

Redesigning how specific tasks get done

Sourcing teams bring RFx development, market intelligence, and negotiation scenarios. Contracting teams bring live agreements, risk analysis, and term drafting. The workflows being redesigned are the actual workflows, not hypothetical ones.

◆ Tool Building

Creating the agents and automations that execute the work

The tools being built are the actual tools practitioners will use Monday morning. No documentation handoffs. No pre-built solutions delivered to them. They build, own, understand, and can evolve what they create.

How It Works

Three Phases of Capability Building

A structured engagement that evolves from foundation training to self-sustaining capability. Click each phase to explore.

1

Foundation Fluency

Learn by doing on real work. Build simple agents. Change how tasks get done.

What Practitioners Learn

  • Chatbot mastery and prompt engineering
  • Custom agent building
  • AI-augmented research and drafting
  • Workflow redesign for AI integration

What Gets Delivered

  • Validated capability — clear visibility into who "gets it"
  • Working tools built by the team, not delivered to them
  • Changed workflows already in use
  • Foundation for deeper capability building
Real Example

A contracting practitioner doesn't learn "how to write prompts" in the abstract. They learn by prompting AI to draft a liability limitation clause for a cloud services agreement they're actually negotiating. They refine the prompt. They build an agent that codifies their best version. They redesign their drafting workflow to use it.

2

Augmented Execution

Develop proficiency through hands-on collaboration on live deliverables.

What This Looks Like

  • Working alongside teams on live work — co-creating in real time
  • Auditing and refining as work happens
  • Pushing to more sophisticated applications as fluency grows
  • Preventing dependency through demonstration, not execution

What Determines Success

  • Curiosity and critical thinking from practitioners
  • Willingness to challenge old workflows
  • Authority to implement changes
  • Organizational commitment to sustain the effort
How It's Different

This isn't coaching from the sidelines. Expert support works alongside the team day-to-day on actual deliverables — showing how to do it while doing it together, not doing it for them. The engagement evolves from training to performance partnership.

3

Advanced Capability Development

High-potential individuals learn methodical app development using agentic coding platforms.

What Gets Built

  • Custom workflows and automation
  • Multi-agent systems for complex S2C processes
  • Sophisticated applications without engineering backgrounds
  • The internal capability engine for the organization

Who Gets Selected

  • Performance in Phases 1 and 2 determines selection
  • Based on adoption speed and use case sophistication
  • How far they push beyond what's taught
  • This isn't for everyone — but those who get here become the engine
The Focus

This isn't vibe coding. The focus is on methodology: how to approach app development with AI coding tools, how to architect solutions, how to iterate and debug, how to build maintainable systems. These practitioners aren't just using AI — they're building the solutions the organization needs without external consultants.

The Choice

Where Should the Learning Happen?

Organizations face a fundamental decision about where to deploy AI investment. Toggle between the two approaches.

Invest in Consultants First

● Paying for someone else to learn AI skills on your budget
● Immediate capability but no knowledge transfer
● Ongoing dependency for execution
● Cost scales linearly with usage
● Capability evaporates when consultants leave
● Black box tools and methodologies
● Practitioners become administrators of consultant-delivered work
Permanent operating expense for capability that should be a capital investment.

Invest in People First

● Your team develops the capability, you retain the knowledge
● Capability building takes time but compounds
● Knowledge stays inside the organization
● Cost decreases over time as team matures
● Practitioners become more valuable — career development and retention
● Tools built by the team, understood by the team, evolved by the team
● Strategic flexibility to adapt as business needs change
Cost avoidance at scale. Every process that shifts from consultant-dependent to internally executed is margin recaptured.
